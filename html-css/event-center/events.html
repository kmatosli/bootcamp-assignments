History

Roman Spain: Empire, Infrastructure & Power

For more than six centuries, the Iberian Peninsula was the western frontier of the Roman Empire. The roads, aqueducts, theatres, and cities that survive today are not ruins — they're arguments about how civilization is built.

This journey traces the Roman presence from Tarragona's amphitheatre to Mérida's magnificent theatre, with a private archaeologist guide who understands both the engineering and the politics.

Duration8 Days
Group SizeMax 10
PriceFrom $3,200
CitiesTarragona · Mérida · Córdoba · Segovia
  • Private archaeologist-led tour of Mérida's Roman theatre
  • Walk original Roman road segments outside Córdoba
  • Evening lecture: Rome vs. modern European infrastructure
  • Segovia aqueduct — 2,000 years, no mortar
  • Layered city walk: Roman → Moorish → Christian Córdoba
  • Curated evening: local hammam & dinner off the beaten path

This journey connects the texts to the terrain — and the terrain to the trauma of Civil War, exile, and the literary genius that emerged from both.

Flamenco: History, Movement & Cultural Identity

This is not a tourist dance class. Flamenco is a living record of cultural collision — Roma tradition, Moorish musical scales, Andalusian grief, and centuries of marginalization transformed into devastating art.

You'll learn the movements. You'll also understand why they mean what they mean.

Theatre & Literature

The Golden Age: Spain's Theatrical Revolution

While Shakespeare was reshaping English drama, Spain was experiencing its own theatrical explosion — and almost no one in the English-speaking world knows it. Lope de Vega wrote over 400 surviving plays. Calderón de la Barca's La vida es sueño is one of the most philosophically profound works in Western literature. Tirso de Molina invented Don Juan. This is the trip for everyone who studied Spanish literature and was never taught any of this.

Built around the Festival Internacional de Teatro Clásico de Almagro — the world's largest Golden Age theatre festival, held every July in a 17th-century Corral de Comedias that has been in continuous operation since 1628 — this journey combines live performance with the cities, histories, and ideas that produced Spain's greatest literary century.

DatesJuly 5–13, 2026
Duration9 Days
Group SizeMax 10
PriceFrom $3,400
CitiesMadrid · Alcalá de Henares · Toledo · Almagro
  • Attend 2–3 live performances at Almagro festival — Calderón, Lope de Vega, Tirso de Molina — in the Corral de Comedias built in 1628
  • Pre-show discussion each evening: what you're about to see, why it matters, what the playwright was arguing
  • Madrid's Barrio de las Letras — the literary quarter where Cervantes, Lope de Vega, and Quevedo all lived within blocks of each other
  • Alcalá de Henares — Cervantes' birthplace and its own Classical Theatre Festival, with a Corral de Comedias still in use
  • Toledo — Calderón's city: the layered Roman, Moorish, and Christian capital that shaped his auto sacramentales
  • National Museum of Theatre (Almagro) — costumes, stage machinery, and manuscripts from the Golden Age
  • Evening salon: The Siglo de Oro as Spain's Shakespeare moment — and why American universities ignore it
  • Pre-trip reading list: one play each from Calderón (La vida es sueño), Lope (Fuenteovejuna), Tirso (El burlador de Sevilla), and Cervantes' entremeses

Art & Visual Culture

Seeing Spain: An Art Appreciation Journey

Most people walk past Las Meninas in 45 seconds. They glance at Goya's Black Paintings and keep moving. They stand in front of El Greco's Burial of the Count of Orgaz and feel vaguely impressed without knowing why. They leave the Prado overwhelmed — and no richer for it.

This journey is built around one radical idea: you don't need an art history degree to truly see a masterpiece. You need someone to slow you down and teach you the language. Once you understand WHY Velázquez's brushwork was revolutionary, WHY Goya painted his darkest works in secret on his own walls, WHY El Greco deliberately rearranged Toledo's skyline — you can never un-see it. Every museum you visit for the rest of your life changes.

And then you pick up a brush yourself. Not to become an artist. To train your eye. Because trying to paint what you see forces you to actually look at it — and that changes everything.

DatesAug 24–Sep 1, 2026
Duration9 Days
Group SizeMax 8
PriceFrom $3,500
CitiesMadrid · Toledo · Bilbao
  • The Prado — Done Right: Not a highlights rush. Six masterworks. 30 minutes each. Velázquez, Goya, El Greco, Bosch — with a guide who teaches you to see, not just look
  • Las Meninas decoded: Why did Velázquez paint himself into a royal portrait? What is he actually saying about power, art, and the nature of seeing?
  • Goya's Black Paintings: He painted these secretly on his own dining room walls — they were never meant to be exhibited. What does that tell us about the artist?
  • Reina Sofía — Guernica with full Civil War context: Then a Cubist sketch workshop — you try Picasso's technique of decomposing what you see
  • Toledo — El Greco in context: See The Burial of the Count of Orgaz in the actual church it was painted for, not a museum. Then stand outside and look at Toledo the way he painted it
  • Studio Day — Pick up the brush: Local Madrid artist's studio. Watercolor or oil pastel — your choice. Velázquez light-and-shadow OR Goya dramatic contrast. All skill levels. The point is to look, not to produce a masterpiece
  • Bilbao — Guggenheim as architecture: How Frank Gehry designed a building that changed a dying industrial city. The art inside AND the building itself as art
  • Outdoor sketching session, Toledo: Try to draw what El Greco painted. Even stick figures. The attempt forces your eye to see what it normally skips
  • Pre-trip reading: One essay on each artist — accessible, not academic. You arrive knowing just enough to be dangerous

Women Only · Wellness

Vivir Bien: A Women's Wellness Journey Through Andalusia

You have spent $400 at Aire Ancient Baths for a single hammam session. You have booked spa treatments at hotel prices that should embarrass everyone involved. You have sat in a Gate1 bus for eight hours and arrived somewhere beautiful too exhausted to appreciate it.

This trip is the antidote to all of that.

Spain has one of the oldest and most layered wellness traditions in the Western world — Roman thermal springs built 2,000 years ago, Moorish hammam culture that turned bathing into a social art, olive oil therapy rooted in Andalusian agricultural heritage, and a daily philosophy of vivir bien — living well — that has nothing to do with productivity and everything to do with presence. Most travelers walk past all of it. You are going to live inside it for seven days.

This is not a yoga retreat. There is no meditation gong. There is no juice cleanse. This is what Spanish women have known for centuries — that the body deserves unhurried attention, that beauty comes from the earth you live on, and that pleasure is not indulgence. It is cultural intelligence.

DatesOct 3–9, 2026
Duration7 Days
WhoWomen only · Max 10
PriceFrom $3,200
CitiesGranada · Córdoba · Ronda
  • Hammam Al Ándalus Granada — full ritual on arrival night: warm pool, hot pool, cold plunge, kessa scrub, ghassoul clay mask. The authentic Moorish sequence, not the tourist version
  • The Alhambra — slowly. Two hours. Four rooms. No rushing. You sit in the Nasrid Palaces long enough to actually feel them
  • Balneario thermal springs — ancient mineral waters that Romans built, Arabs rebuilt, and Spaniards have bathed in continuously for 2,000 years
  • Olive oil morning in Jaén — visit an almazara (traditional olive press), taste single-estate oils, then receive a full olive oil body treatment using the oil you just tasted. You understand it before it touches your skin
  • Hammam Al Ándalus Córdoba — different hammam, different city, different Moorish tradition. Compare the two
  • The Mezquita at your own pace — one of the most spiritually overwhelming buildings in the world. Sit in it. Don't rush through it
  • Ronda — the most dramatically beautiful town in Spain, perched above a 400-foot gorge. One full free afternoon. Walk, sit, read, watch the light change. This is the paseo as philosophy
  • Flamenco movement session — not performance, not tourist show. A private session with a local dancer focused on embodied expression and emotional release through movement
  • Thalassotherapy — Atlantic coastal spa using warmed seawater circuits, marine mud, and sea air. Spain has 2,000km of coastline and has been doing this for centuries
  • The vivir bien table — every group dinner is unhurried, every dish explained, every wine chosen. No phones. Mediterranean eating as it was meant to be practiced
  • Zero solo traveler upcharge. Come alone. You will not feel alone for one single hour of this trip

Architecture

Barcelona: The Full Architectural Argument

Every tourist sees the Sagrada Família. Every tourist photographs Casa Batlló. Every tourist climbs Park Güell and takes the same picture. And almost every tourist leaves Barcelona having heard one person talking in a room full of geniuses.

Barcelona has been having a 150-year architectural argument — about what buildings should do, what they should say, and who they should serve. Gaudí is the most famous voice. But Lluís Domènech i Montaner may have been the better architect. Josep Puig i Cadafalch was their direct rival. Ildefons Cerdà designed the grid that every building sits inside. Enric Miralles answered them all from the 21st century. And Jean Nouvel asked a completely different question with glass and aluminum.

This trip is the whole conversation. And in 2026, with the completion of the Sagrada Família's Tower of Jesus making it the world's tallest church, there has never been a better moment to understand what Gaudí was actually arguing.

DatesApr 19–25, 2026
Duration7 Days
Group SizeMax 10
PriceFrom $3,300
CityBarcelona (exclusively)
  • Sagrada Família — with the laboratory first: Before the cathedral, the Colònia Güell Crypt — the UNESCO World Heritage Site where Gaudí worked out every structural principle he later used at Sagrada Família. On a typical weekday you share it with six people. This is where the masterpiece was invented
  • The Bloc de la Discòrdia — read the whole argument: Four architects, four philosophies, one block of Passeig de Gràcia. Casa Amatller's blacksmith rabbits and glassblowing frogs versus Gaudí's House of Bones. Most tourists photograph one building. Your group reads the debate
  • Recinte Modernista de Sant Pau — the building tourists should be queuing for: 27 Art Nouveau pavilions, UNESCO World Heritage, a ten-minute walk from Sagrada Família. Arguably more technically dazzling than anything Gaudí built. Almost entirely uncrowded
  • Palau de la Música Catalana — attended as a concert, not a tour: Domènech i Montaner designed this building to be experienced with music. The stained glass ceiling, the sculptural columns, the acoustics. You attend an evening performance. The building performs
  • Casa Vicens — Gaudí's first house, his most surprising: Before the curves. Before the religion. Mudéjar influence, bold ceramic tiles, botanical interior painting. In the residential Gràcia neighborhood. Almost nobody finds it
  • Torre Bellesguard — Gaudí's medieval castle: Built on the ruins of a 15th-century royal residence. Straight lines — rare for Gaudí. Gothic verticality. Medieval Catalan history embedded in the foundations. Practically empty on any given day
  • Born Market underground excavations: Beneath a 19th-century iron market, archaeologists found an entire neighborhood destroyed in 1714 when Spain conquered Catalonia. The streets, homes, and artifacts the victors never documented. This is where Catalan identity becomes visceral and architectural at the same time
  • Bunkers del Carmel at sunset: Anti-aircraft battery from the Spanish Civil War. 360° views — Sagrada Família, Montjuïc, the Mediterranean, the mountains. Free. Used by locals. The best view in Barcelona and almost no tourists know it
  • Mercat de Santa Caterina: Enric Miralles' wildly undulating mosaic roof — the 21st century's answer to Gaudí's trencadís. This is where the neighborhood actually shops. La Boqueria is the tourist market. This is the real one
  • The Eixample grid — Cerdà's radical social vision: Every building in Barcelona sits inside Ildefons Cerdà's 1860 urban plan. He designed the chamfered corners so light could reach every apartment. He designed interior courtyards as public gardens. The grid itself is a political argument about equality. Almost no tourist knows his name
  • Torres Glòries — Jean Nouvel's glass argument: 472 feet of multi-colored glass and aluminum asking what 21st-century Barcelona says in response to 19th-century Modernisme. The Pritzker jury cited his ability to rupture scale and manipulate light
  • Sketching session: One building, 45 minutes, whatever medium you choose. The act of trying to draw what you see forces your eye to notice what it skips. You leave with a sketch and a permanently changed way of seeing buildings
